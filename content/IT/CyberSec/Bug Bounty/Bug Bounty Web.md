# 10-Week Bug Bounty Hunting Plan for a Non-Technical Beginner

## Week 1: Getting Started – Bug Bounty Basics & IDOR

- **Intro to Bug Bounties:** Give her a high-level idea of how platforms like HackerOne work (find a security bug in a website, report it, and get paid). Keep it super simple and motivating – she can earn money by poking around websites, no deep programming required.
- **What is IDOR?** Introduce _Insecure Direct Object Reference (IDOR)_ in plain language. For example, “If you change a number in a URL or request and suddenly you’re seeing someone else’s data, that’s an IDOR.” Emphasize that IDOR is a common bug and often rewarded well (average bounty around **$1,200** for IDOR). It’s a quick win because it doesn’t require coding – just curiosity.
- **Tools Setup (Keep it Minimal):** Help her set up very basic tools. Start with just a web browser and an account on HackerOne (so she can read about programs and submit bugs). If she’s willing, install **Burp Suite** (free edition) as an intercepting proxy – this lets her pause and modify web requests easily. Also consider the **Autorize** extension for Burp, which can automatically check requests for IDOR by repeating them with another user’s token. (No need to understand how it works under the hood – just “set and forget” to help catch IDORs.)
- **Practice IDOR Testing:** Walk her through a simple IDOR example in a safe environment. For instance, use a demo or practice lab where she has two accounts. Show a URL like `example.com/profile?user=123` – then change `123` to `124` and see if she can view another user’s profile. The moment she sees private data that shouldn’t be accessible, congratulate her – that’s an IDOR! Let her try this on a couple of pages.
- **Goal:** By the end of Week 1, she should grasp the concept of IDOR and feel comfortable trying basic parameter tweaks to find unauthorized access. The goal is **not** to hunt on real targets yet, but to build confidence. If she’s eager, she can start looking at a real site for any obvious IDOR (e.g. a poorly protected user profile page), but it’s fine if she sticks to practice.
- **Optional Practice Lab:** If she wants hands-on practice, use an interactive lab like PortSwigger’s IDOR exercise. It’s a simple step-by-step environment to reinforce the idea of modifying object IDs to access someone else’s info (great for someone who learns by doing).

## Week 2: Low-Hanging Fruit – Reflected XSS Made Easy

- **Reflected XSS in Simple Terms:** Explain _Reflected Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)_ without jargon. For example: “Some websites will echo back whatever you input. If they don’t handle it safely, you can insert a bit of script and cause the site to do something like show an alert box.” She doesn’t need to know JavaScript deeply – just that adding a `<script>alert(1)</script>` into a text field might trigger a popup if the site is vulnerable. That popup means _she found a bug!_
- Emphasize that **XSS is the #1 reported bug** on bug bounty platforms. It’s super common, which means many websites still have it. This is good news for a beginner: it’s everywhere and fairly straightforward to test. Emphasize results: “We’re going to try a few places to input a script and see if we get an alert – no deep theory, just trial and error.”
- **Tools/Techniques:** Show her how to test for XSS using just the browser. For instance, have her find a search box or any text input on a target site and type in something obvious like `\<script>alert(1)\</script>` (or use an XSS cheat sheet payload). If the site reflects that text back on the page, it might execute the script. To save effort, she can also use tools: **OWASP ZAP** (with the automated scanner) or a specialized XSS finder like **XSStrike** can help fuzz many parameters quickly. Stress that using these tools doesn’t require her to know the details – it’s clicking a scan button and waiting for results.
- **Practice Activity:** Let her practice on a safe site first so she sees what a success looks like. For example, the PortSwigger Academy has a simple Reflected XSS lab where the goal is literally to pop an alert. Have her solve that – it will boost her confidence. Then, move to a real bug bounty target (maybe start with a relatively simple one, like a public program known for lots of content pages). Guide her to try the same `<script>alert(1)</script>` trick in various inputs (search bars, query parameters in the URL, feedback forms, etc.). She should not worry about understanding why it works; just focus on “did my alert show up?”
- **Goal:** By the end of Week 2, she should be able to test for reflected XSS on her own and recognize when it happens. The **goal this week** is to attempt XSS on at least one real target program. If she’s lucky, she might even find one that hasn’t been reported yet. But even if she doesn’t get a hit, she’s building a methodology: try easy XSS payloads everywhere. Success for this week is measured by her comfort in doing these tests without supervision.

## Week 3: Expanding Horizons – Basic Recon & Info Disclosure

- **Basic Recon (No Overwhelm):** Introduce the idea of “reconnaissance” in a very accessible way. Clarify that recon is just a fancy word for exploring and gathering info about the target. Encourage her natural laziness here: _easy wins only_. This means: visit the target website(s) and look around thoroughly. For example, check the homepage, see if there’s a `/blog`, `/admin`, or weird-looking URLs. Have her Google the company or use the site’s search feature to discover hidden pages. No need to run complex scripts yet – just manually clicking around and observing.
- **Information Disclosure Bugs:** Explain that sometimes websites accidentally leave sensitive information out in the open. This could be error messages that reveal software versions, config files accessible publicly, or debug pages. Give a couple of examples: “Imagine the site has a file like `config.php.bak` that anyone can download – that’s a bug.” Tell her to keep an eye out for anything that looks like it doesn’t belong on a public site (stack traces, `.git/` folders, etc.). These _info disclosure_ issues are considered low-hanging fruit too – not super sexy, but bounty programs do pay for leaked secrets or credentials.
- **Easy Tools for Recon:** Since she’s not coding, use point-and-click tools. Show her **DirBuster/Dirsearch** (or OWASP ZAP’s spider) to brute-force common files and directories – basically, the tool will automatically try a list of common filenames (like `admin.php`, `backup.zip`, `/.env`) while she relaxes. Another lazy trick: **Google Dorking** – use Google to search the domain for keywords like “password” or “error” or file extensions like `ext:log` (sometimes sensitive info is indexed!). This leverages Google’s power so she doesn’t have to manually find stuff.
- **Practice Activity:** Pick a target with a broad scope (lots of subdomains or pages) that is in a bug bounty program. Have her do a short “treasure hunt”: find one interesting thing that could be a vulnerability. For instance, visit `target.com/robots.txt` (maybe it lists hidden paths), try adding `/admin` or `/backup` to the URL to see if something comes up, or run a DirBuster scan and see if it finds a strange file. Also, if she ever triggers an error message, copy it down – it might reveal versions or file paths that could be useful. The key is **observation**. If she finds an openly accessible file with sensitive info (say, a database dump or credentials), that’s a definite reportable bug (and likely a quick payout). Even a smaller find, like an error page showing server details, is worth noting.
- **Goal:** By the end of Week 3, she should have at least one _finding_ from recon. Ideally, this is something she can report – for example, a publicly accessible sensitive file or some credentials/API keys exposed. But even if it’s just an insight (like “I noticed the site uses WordPress version X” or “there’s a weird `/test` page that requires no login”), it’s progress. She’s learning to think like a hacker by exploring. The goal is to instill a habit: whenever she approaches a new target, she’ll naturally poke around for low-effort clues before diving into testing. Plus, if she _did_ uncover something like a leaked secret, she might already have her first valid bug to report.

## Week 4: First Real Bug Hunt – Open Redirects & Reporting a Bug

- **Open Redirect – Easiest Exploit Ever:** Teach her about open redirects, which are basically when a website lets you bounce a user to an arbitrary URL based on a parameter. Non-technical description: “Some sites have links like `logout?next=URL` or `redirect=URL`. If the site isn’t careful, you can change that URL to `http://evil.com` and the site will send users there.” It’s a simple bug and usually low severity, but it’s so easy to check that it’s worth trying everywhere. Point out common parameter names to watch for: `?redirect=`, `?next=`, `?url=` are big giveaways. Many programs will pay a small bounty for a valid open redirect (average payouts are on the lower side, a few hundred dollars or less), but it still counts as a bounty and gets her on the board.
- **Applying What She’s Learned:** This week, the strategy is to **combine all the low-hanging fruit techniques** on a real target program with the aim of getting a report in. Have her choose one target (perhaps one she reconned last week or a new one that looks promising and not overly hardened). She should spend her 4 hours systematically doing the easy tests:
    
    - Try IDOR on any authenticated function she can access (if the program has a login/signup).
    - Try her XSS payload in every input field or parameter she can find.
    - Look for open redirect parameters and test those.
    - Double-check for any information disclosures (maybe something she noted earlier or a new discovery like a forgotten subdomain).
- **No New Tools Needed:** Just use Burp or the browser as she’s been doing. If she wants to be thorough with open redirects without too much work, there’s a tool **OpenRedirex** that can scan a list of URLs for open redirect issues – but simply manually testing a few suspected parameters is often enough. The key is to actually attempt _everything_ she learned so far on this one target. Her good memory will help here – she can recall the patterns and param names to try without having to re-read docs each time.
- **First Bug Submission:** With a bit of luck, by now she has something concrete – maybe a reflected XSS that popped up on a less obvious page, or an open redirect, or a file she found via recon. Work with her to **write a clear report** for submission. Keep it short and sweet: “**Summary:** what the bug is and what it impacts. **Steps to reproduce:** 1-2-3 how to see the bug. **Impact:** why it could be bad (e.g., phishing via open redirect, or XSS can steal cookies, etc.).” Encourage her to be clear and not overly technical – being understandable is key. For example, an open redirect report can simply say: “The `next` parameter on `/login` allows arbitrary redirects. I was able to redirect users to `evil.com` by visiting `https://site.com/login?next=http://evil.com`.” That’s it. No fancy terminology needed.
- **Goal:** By the end of Week 4, the **primary goal** is to have submitted her first bug report on a bug bounty platform. If the bug is valid, this will likely lead to her **first payout** 🎉. (It might take some days to get triaged and paid, but getting it in by week 4 is a huge milestone.) Even if, say, the report comes back as a duplicate or “informative,” it’s okay – the process of finding and reporting is the real achievement here. She’s now gone through the full cycle once. From here on, everything will feel easier.

## Week 5: After the First Bounty – Stored XSS and CSRF (Stepping Up Slightly)

- **Build on Success:** Start the week by recapping her win. If her first report got accepted and paid out, celebrate that! 🎊 Use that momentum to gently introduce slightly deeper concepts, but reassure her it’s more of the same “try it and see” approach. No need to suddenly dive into hardcore theory.
- **Stored XSS (Persistent XSS):** Let her know that XSS isn’t just in reflections – sometimes the script gets stored in the website’s database (for example, in a comment or profile field) and affects every user who sees it. This is called stored XSS and often has a bigger impact (and bounty) because it can hit many users at once. The good news: finding stored XSS is similar to reflected – she just tries inputting her `<script>alert(1)</script>` in places that might get saved (comment boxes, user profile settings, etc.). If she can’t think of where, tell her to imagine “Anywhere I can enter text that other people might see later – that’s a candidate.” Have her give it a shot on a target that has user-to-user interaction (maybe a forum or comment section in scope). It’s still low-hanging in terms of skill, just requires patience to check if the script triggers when viewing the content later. (She can even comment on her own post and refresh the page to see if the alert appears.)
- **Intro to CSRF:** Introduce _Cross-Site Request Forgery_ as a concept, but keep it high-level. For example: “Some sites forget to check that a request actually came from a logged-in user’s browser. If so, a bad guy could trick you into clicking a link that performs an action on your account without you knowing.” In practice, explain that if a site lacks CSRF protection, an attacker could create a fake form or link that, when the victim is logged in, causes actions like changing password, transferring money, etc. She doesn’t need to know about CSRF tokens deeply – just tell her the _symptom_: if a sensitive action (like changing email or making a post) can be done by simply visiting a URL or loading a form from another site, that’s a CSRF bug.
- **Simple CSRF Testing:** Show her how to do a lazy CSRF test. One way is using a tool like Burp’s CSRF PoC generator, which will give an HTML form for any request. But even without that, she can take a request (say, updating a user profile) and turn it into a basic HTML `<form>` that submits to the target. Then open that HTML while logged in and see if it executes the action. If the action succeeds without any special token, voila – likely a CSRF vulnerability. Many modern sites have protections, but occasionally smaller programs forget, and that’s another quick payday for relatively little work.
- **Practice Activity:** This week is about expanding her testing to these new categories on targets she’s comfortable with. For stored XSS, maybe use a demo like an intentionally vulnerable forum (if available) to illustrate, then attempt on a real target. For CSRF, it might be harder to find on modern sites, so it’s okay if it’s just a discussion topic and a quick check on one or two forms. If she has a website where she reported an IDOR or XSS already, have her double-check if forms there have CSRF protection (sometimes one bug indicates other sloppy security practices).
- **Goal:** By the end of Week 5, she should be aware of stored XSS and CSRF as concepts and feel that they’re just slight variations of what she’s already doing – **not** whole new skills to fear. The goal isn’t necessarily to find a stored XSS or CSRF within this week (though that would be awesome), but rather to broaden her mindset. Now when she sees an input, she’ll think “Could this be a stored XSS?” and when she sees a form, “Is this protected from CSRF?”. This awareness will pay off in future hunts. Essentially, we’re adding a couple more **items to her lazy hacker checklist** – still simple tests, just new scenarios.

## Week 6: Higher Payout Hunting – Intro to SSRF (Server-Side Request Forgery)

- **Why SSRF?** Shift her focus now to a bug type that’s a bit more advanced but can be extremely rewarding. Explain _Server-Side Request Forgery (SSRF)_ as: “Some features let the server fetch data from a URL you give it. If the server isn’t restricted, you can make it fetch things it shouldn’t – like internal services or cloud metadata – by supplying a crafty URL.” Put simply, **SSRF tricks the website into connecting to somewhere on your behalf**. It’s less common to find than XSS/IDOR, but when found, companies pay big because it can lead to critical access. (Fun fact to motivate her: SSRF made it into the OWASP Top 10 due to its impact, even if incidence was low.)
- **Keep It Simple:** She doesn’t need to understand internal networks deeply. Just tell her: “If you ever see a feature where you can input a link or an address (like import an image from URL, webhook tester, PDF export from a link, etc.), that’s a place to try SSRF.” The test is straightforward: instead of a normal URL, give it something like `http://127.0.0.1` (which usually points to the server itself) or a unique URL from a service like **Burp Collaborator** or **Webhook.site**. If the server is vulnerable, it might reveal internal content or you’ll see a ping on your collaborator server indicating the server tried to fetch your URL. No coding – just copying a special URL and checking for a response.
- **Tools for SSRF:** Introduce her to **Burp Collaborator Client** (built into Burp Suite) in a very hand-holding way. Essentially: “Click this button, get a weird URL, paste it into the field on the target site, and click submit. Then see if Burp says the server touched that URL.” That’s it. Alternatively, use an external free service like `webhook.site` which gives a URL and shows any requests received. The concept is the same. This way she doesn’t have to set up any server herself.
- **Practice Mindset:** SSRF can be hard to simulate without a real feature to test, but if you know of any easy sandbox or practice (for example, some CTFs or labs have a basic SSRF challenge), that can help illustrate it. If not, maybe find a low-stakes target that has an image upload via URL or an RSS feed importer – something where she can input a URL. Let her try hitting an internal address (like `http://localhost` or `http://169.254.169.254` which is a common cloud metadata URL) and see if the response delays or returns interesting data. Even if she doesn’t get a clear “Got it!” moment, walking through the process demystifies it.
- **Goal:** By the end of Week 6, she should know **what SSRF is and how to attempt it**. The goal here is educational: add another powerful bug class to her toolkit. If she stumbles upon a possible SSRF in the wild, she’ll now know, “Oh, I can try giving it a collaborator URL and see what happens.” We’re planting the seed for higher-severity findings. Even though this is a bit more technical than previous weeks, frame it as another _lazy test_: just input a special URL and wait for results. No complex exploitation needed if the vulnerability is there.

## Week 7: Revisiting Classics – SQL Injection (with Automation) and Other Oldies

- **SQL Injection Simplified:** Bring up _SQL Injection (SQLi)_ – the legendary bug that’s less common today but not extinct. Explain it in a story-like way: “Websites often talk to databases. If they do it unsafely, an attacker can sneak in a database query and get data they shouldn’t.” Use a super simple example: say a login form that might be vulnerable – if she inputs `' OR 1=1--` as the username, and it logs her in without a password, that’s SQLi. She doesn’t need to write SQL queries from scratch; just know that certain characters (like a quote `'` or semicolon `;`) might break the query and cause errors or unwanted behavior. In practical terms, tell her whenever she sees a text field or a URL parameter that looks like it might be used in a database query (especially if she gets an error when inputting weird stuff), that’s a place to try some basic SQL injection payloads.
- **Leverage Tools – SQLMap:** Introduce **SQLMap**, the ultimate lazy hacker tool for SQLi. SQLMap automates the entire injection process. Make sure she understands the philosophy: _you_ don’t have to be the database guru – SQLMap will do the heavy lifting. Give a quick demo: if she found a page that might be injectable (say an old-fashioned product catalog page with `?id=123`), she can run a one-liner with SQLMap and it will try a bunch of injections and even dump data if successful. This saves her from typing out different payloads manually. It’s okay if running SQLMap feels a bit technical (it’s command-line); if that’s a barrier, she can skip it and just stick to trying a few classic `'` or `OR 1=1` inputs by hand. The main point: **know that automation exists** to exploit SQLi.
- **Other “Old but Gold” Bugs:** Briefly mention a couple of other simple vulnerabilities she might occasionally check for: for example, **Local File Inclusion (LFI)** or **Path Traversal** (when a URL includes a filename or path, try adding `../` to see if you can break out of a directory), or **Command Injection** (if she ever finds a form that might run system commands, like a ping utility – try `;` to chain a new command). These are rarer and more technical, so don’t deep dive – just let her know that as a bug bounty hunter she’ll eventually hear these terms. If she remembers one day that “maybe I can add `../../etc/passwd` to this URL to see system files”, that’s a plus. It’s more of a heads-up than a must-do-now.
- **Practice Activity:** If possible, use a vulnerable practice site (like DVWA or an older Juice Shop challenge) to show a basic SQLi. For instance, show how a search box might reveal an SQL error when you type a quote, indicating a SQLi vulnerability. If she’s comfortable, let her use SQLMap against a known test target to see the automation in action (seeing SQLMap dump a user table is pretty motivating!). On a real bug bounty target, finding an SQLi will be tough as a beginner, so it’s more likely she won’t encounter one immediately. But encourage her to incorporate a quick `'` test in her routine: whenever she’s testing a parameter for XSS or IDOR, throwing in a single quote to see if an error comes up is an almost zero-effort addition. You never know – some less-maintained site could be vulnerable.
- **Goal:** By the end of Week 7, she should **not be intimidated by terms like SQLi**. The goal is confidence and awareness rather than actually finding a SQLi bug (which would be awesome but is gravy). She should now feel like, “Okay, if I suspect a SQL injection, I know a couple of things to try or a tool to run.” Essentially, this week rounds out her knowledge of the “OWASP classics,” making her a more well-rounded hunter. She’s mostly still aiming for easy wins, but she won’t be blindsided if she stumbles on an input that needs a SQLi test. Every simple check added to her arsenal is another chance to find a bug.

## Week 8: Working Smarter – Automation & Scoping Out More Targets

- **Scaling Up with Automation:** Now that she has a variety of tricks, teach her how to cover more ground with less manual effort (the lazy hacker’s dream!). Introduce the idea of using automation to scan targets for known issues or to enumerate more of the attack surface. Reiterate that many bug bounty hunters use scripts not because they love coding, but because it saves time and finds easy stuff while they sleep. This is perfectly in line with her style – let the tools do the boring work, then she can swoop in when something interesting is found.
- **Subdomain Enumeration:** Explain that big companies often have dozens or hundreds of subdomains (e.g., `api.target.com`, `beta.target.com`, `old.target.com`). Bugs often hide on these less-visited subdomains which the main security team might not monitor closely. Teach her an easy way to get subdomains: using tools like **Sublist3r, Amass**, or even web services like **crt.sh** (certificate transparency logs). For minimal effort, she can use an online tool (like PassiveTotal or SecurityTrails) that lists known subdomains for a domain without setting up anything. The idea is to quickly get a list of many subdomains to potentially test, increasing her chances of finding a low-hanging bug on one of them. Many newbies get their first big find on some forgotten subdomain with old software. This is a “wider net” approach.
- **Using Nuclei for Quick Wins:** Introduce **Nuclei**, a popular automation tool that uses templates to find common vulnerabilities. Frame it like this: “Nuclei is a bunch of pre-scripted tests written by other hackers. You just point it at a target, and it will check for hundreds of known issues (default passwords, outdated software, config files, etc.) for you.” Setting up Nuclei might require command line, but it’s straightforward if you guide her step-by-step. Emphasize she doesn’t have to write any code – just run existing templates. For example, she can run a collection of “misconfiguration” templates across all those subdomains she found. Maybe one of them has an open Jenkins instance with admin/admin creds – that’s an instant payout and she wouldn’t have found it manually!
- **Safe Automation Practices:** One quick note – ensure she checks a program’s policy to see if scanning is allowed. Many bug bounty programs are okay with it (especially passive or authenticated scans), but a few might ask not to hit them with heavy tools. Since she’s only spending 4 hours a week, she’s unlikely to go overboard. Just advise using rate limits or spreading out scans to not overwhelm any single target’s servers. Basically, “automate lazily”: run things in the background at a gentle pace.
- **Practice Activity:** This week, have her pick one or two programs that have broad scope (e.g., `*.target.com` in scope). Help her gather subdomains for one of them using a simple method (even a quick Google like `"site:*.target.com"` or a tool output). Once she has a list (even if it’s 10-20 subdomains to start), show her how to feed that into Nuclei or another scanner. For instance, run Nuclei’s “common vulnerabilities” template set against each subdomain. When the scan is done, go through the results with her. She might see “possible XSS” or “directory listing found” – teach her to interpret which ones are worth a closer look. This turns hunting into more of a review job – let the tool find hints, and she verifies if any are real issues.
- **Goal:** By the end of Week 8, she should be comfortable with the idea of automating parts of her workflow. The concrete goal is to have run an automated scan (or a few) and ideally identified **at least one promising finding** from it. For example, maybe Nuclei pointed out an exposed admin panel on `dev.target.com` – next step could be testing a default password there. Even if nothing immediately pans out, she’s learned how to dramatically increase coverage without much extra effort. This sets her up to be a “smart lazy” hacker: the one who writes down a target, runs some tools, and then just focuses on the interesting bits that come out. It’s a force multiplier for her 4-hours-a-week constraint.

## Week 9: Learning from Others & Sharpening the Saw

- **Review and Reflect:** Nine weeks in, she’s amassed a lot of tips and tricks. This week, shift focus from actively hunting to **learning and refining strategy** (a bit of a breather, which suits her style). Explain that one of the best ways to get better (without heavy studying) is to read write-ups of bugs other people have found. Since she has a good memory, reading bug bounty reports will stick with her and give her new ideas to try – effectively letting others do the deep thinking for her.
- **Read 1–2 Write-ups:** Pick a couple of beginner-friendly write-ups (perhaps on Medium or Hacktivity on HackerOne) that showcase how a simple bug was found. Ideally, these should be related to things she’s tried, like an IDOR case study or a neat XSS trick, so she can relate. For example, a story of how a hacker found an IDOR in an API and got paid. As she reads, help her note the key steps or patterns (“They noticed an `user_id` in the API response and tried changing it”). Discuss how she could apply similar steps in her targets. This not only teaches her new techniques but reinforces that the things she’s doing are exactly what successful hackers do – boosting her confidence.
- **Join the Community (Lurking encouraged):** Recommend she joins an online community such as the **/r/bugbounty** subreddit or follows a few bug bounty hunters on Twitter. She doesn’t have to post or engage (since she might not want to dive into discussions), but simply seeing the questions and tips posted can be enlightening. Often people share quick wins or common pitfalls (“Hey, check if X site has a /graphql endpoint – found an IDOR there!”). These bite-sized insights can be golden for a lazy afternoon of hacking.
- **Organize and Strategize:** Since she’s results-oriented, suggest she start maintaining a very simple log or checklist for each target she works on. Nothing fancy – even a text file or Google Sheet where for each target she notes what she has tried (XSS, IDOR, directories scanned, etc.) and what she found. This helps prevent repeating the exact same tests on the same target (unless it’s after new features are added). It also appeals to her memory strength: if she records what worked on one target (“found open redirect on site A”), she might recall to try similar on site B. It’s like building her personal playbook of “go-to moves” per target.
- **Stay Updated, Lazily:** Advise her to allocate maybe 30 minutes of her weekly 4 hours just to skim through a security news or a hacker newsletter. Sometimes new vulnerabilities or techniques emerge. She doesn’t have to fully understand them, but being aware (“Oh, there’s a new WordPress plugin exploit trending”) could inform her what to quickly scan for on relevant targets. Many hackers are lazy in the sense that when a new CVE comes out, they just go hunting for that everywhere – it’s a quick path to bounties. She can do the same once she knows the pattern.
- **Goal:** By the end of Week 9, the goal is qualitative: she should feel more plugged into the bug bounty world and realize she’s not alone – many people share tricks that she can use. If she reads two write-ups, learns one new trick, and organizes her approach, consider it a success. This week is about reinforcing that continuous learning can be low-effort and fun. Importantly, she should come away feeling _inspired_ (“If that person got $3k for a simple IDOR, I can too!”) and perhaps identify one or two new things she wants to try next time she’s hunting. It’s all about working smarter, not harder, and the community knowledge is a big part of that.

## Week 10: Graduation – Harder Targets & Long-Term Growth

- **Tackling Tougher Bugs (Gradually):** In this final week of the initial plan, encourage her to step slightly outside the low-hanging fruit comfort zone – _only if she feels up to it_. She’s had success with easy bugs; now tease some advanced bug types that could be next on her journey. For instance, talk about **business logic flaws**, which are not about missing filters or tokens but about using the application in a way the developers didn’t expect. These often require understanding how an app works (e.g., “What if a user tries to buy something with a negative price?” or “Can a normal user access an admin-only function by changing a parameter beyond just an ID?”). These ideas don’t need coding, just creative misuse of features. Note that such bugs are harder to find but often pay very well and are less likely to be duplicates (because not everyone thinks of them). Given her good memory, she might recall some tricky scenarios from write-ups or discussions and can try those out.
